Let's start by looking at a real job posting. The screenshot below is from the posting's header. The title of the posting is also known as the business title and describes what the job actually does (as opposed to the civil service title, which we'll cover in a moment).


Business Titles

Not all of the business titles listed here are used at every agency, so consider this a list of the most common ones!

The roles with the most senior titles at a City agency are all "appointed" and subject to Mayoral approval. However, roles less senior than a Commissioner are often still open to applicants. Here are the most senior titles:

  • Commissioner
  • First Deputy or Executive Deputy Commissioner
  • Deputy Commissioner
  • Associate Commissioner
  • Assistant Commissioner

The next set of business titles are typically used for middle managers:

  • Executive Director
  • Senior Director
  • Director
  • Deputy Director
  • Assistant Director

Finally, these last titles are used for both non-managerial staff and some senior technical-track roles.

  • Senior Advisor
  • Advisor
  • Senior Policy Advisor
  • Policy Advisor
  • Senior Policy Analyst or Senior Program Manager
  • Policy Analyst or Program Manager

We'll just mention that the most senior business titles in many Mayor's Offices are a bit different from senior agency titles:

  • Director or Executive Director or Commissioner
  • First Deputy Director
  • Deputy Director
  • Associate Director
  • Assistant Director


Meanwhile, although there is a salary range (a big range or a small one), typically the agency has budgeted for a very specific salary number that is rarely negotiable (as in almost never!).




Most of the job posting is the actual job description, including about the agency and team. But an extremely important section is Minimum Qualifications.


Read this section carefully! Your experience and education must meet the exact wording of this section, otherwise your application will be discarded long before the interview round...




This last screenshot is from the footer at the very bottom of the posting. Note the Civil Service Title and Title Classification. If the civil service title is Exempt or Non-Competitive, then you're in luck! You can apply for this job without having to have a civil service title.


But if it says Competitive, you'll likely either need to already have the corresponding civil service title (achieved via an exam) or you'll need to take a civil service exam in the future.


Civil Service Titles

The City of New York's arcane and confusing civil service system is one more thing we can blame Robert Moses for!

Basically, you need to have scored highly on a particular exam (most exams are offered once every few years) to be eligible to be hired into most City jobs (learn more about that here). But there are still thousands of jobs that don't require you to have taken a civil service exam in order to apply for them!

There are also a large number of quasi-governmental organizations, like the NYC Economic Development Corporation, that don't use the civil service system.



The other important item to note is the Posted Until date. If this date is two weeks or less after the Posted On date in the header, then the job is for an internal candidate and it's not worth applying for!





The hiring process for City agencies that use the main hiring website is quite arduous. After applying for a job, it may take between 3 and 12 weeks to hear if you're getting an interview. If you're not selected to move forward, you'll likely never hear back (agencies can receive hundreds of applicants per job). However, some agencies may refer you internally for another position.


If you ultimately receive and accept a job offer, don't quit your current job! It can take another 3 to 9 months for the agency to get final approvals to hire you! Unfortunately, agencies lose many exceptional candidates during this long timeline.
